Chapter 7

Make It Safe

How to make it safe to talk about almost anything

Core Message

When safety is at risk, step out of the content and rebuild safety

People never become defensive about what you're saying—they become defensive because of why they think you're saying it.

Safety is about intent, not content.

Step Out, Make It Safe, Step Back In

1. Notice safety is at risk

Watch for silence or violence

2. Step out of the content

Stop talking about the topic

3. Rebuild safety

Use the skills in this chapter

4. Step back in

Return to the conversation

Two Conditions of Safety

For people to feel safe, they need to know two things about your intent:

Mutual Purpose

The Entrance Condition

Others perceive you're working toward a common outcome

You care about their goals, interests, and values

Mutual Respect

The Continuance Condition

Others perceive that you respect them

Respect is like air—when it's gone, it's all people think about

Mutual Purpose

The entrance condition of dialogue

  • Others believe you're working toward a common outcome
  • You care about their goals, interests, and values (and vice versa)
  • Find a shared goal → you have a reason and climate for talking

A Surefire Mutual Purpose

Human beings have an innate need to be heard and understood. So a great starting mutual purpose is: seek mutual understanding

Mutual Respect

The continuance condition of dialogue

Respect is like air. As long as it's present, nobody thinks about it. But if you take it away, it's all people can think about.

  • When people perceive disrespect → dialogue stops
  • The conversation becomes about defending dignity
  • Watch for signs people are defending dignity (heightened emotions)

Can you respect people you don't respect?

You don't have to respect every element of their character. Find a way to honor their basic humanity. Look for ways you're similar rather than dwelling on differences.

Discussion Question

Think of a time when you felt unsafe in a conversation. Which condition was violated—mutual purpose or mutual respect? How did you know?

Take 4-5 minutes to discuss with the group

Four Skills to Build Safety

Use these to build safety up front or rebuild when it's lost

1

Share Your Good Intent

State what you want for everyone

2

Apologize When Appropriate

Express sorrow for your role in causing pain

3

Contrast to Fix Misunderstandings

Clarify what you don't and do intend

4

Create a Mutual Purpose

Use CRIB when at cross purposes

Skill 1: Share Your Good Intent

If people aren't sure of your intent, they'll assume the worst

It's not enough to have good intentions. The other person must know that's the case.

Example: Oba and Mari

Before: "Mari, I was wondering if we could talk about what happened Friday night..."

After: "Mari, I love you, and I want to make sure we're talking about things that impact our relationship. Our relationship is the most important thing in the world to me. Could we talk?"

Starting with your good intent lays the foundation for safety

Skill 2: Apologize When Appropriate

When you've clearly violated respect

An apology is a statement that sincerely expresses your sorrow for your role in causing—or at least not preventing—pain or difficulty to others.

  • An apology isn't really an apology unless you experience a change in heart
  • You have to give up saving face, being right, or winning
  • You sacrifice a bit of ego to focus on what you really want

Example

"I'm sorry I didn't give you a call when I learned we wouldn't be coming by. You worked all night, and I didn't even explain what happened. I apologize."

Skill 3: Contrast to Fix Misunderstandings

When respect/purpose is violated by misinterpretation

Contrasting is a don't/do statement:

Don't Part

Address concerns that you don't respect them or have malicious purpose

"I don't want you to think..."

Do Part

Clarify what your real intention is

"I do want..."

Example

"I don't want to criticize you or get on your case. I know you're carrying a huge load. I do want us to be able to talk about our concerns so we can build our relationship."

Use Contrasting for Prevention

Don't wait until safety is violated

Example 1: Financial Discussion

"I wanted to talk about how we're managing our finances. I don't want you to think I don't appreciate the time you've taken to keep our account balanced. I do appreciate it. I do, however, have some concerns with how we're using the online banking system."

Example 2: Relationship Issue

"I'd like to talk about something worrying me, and I'm not sure how to handle this conversation. My fear is that I'll damage our relationship, and that's not my intent at all. My goal in bringing this up is to strengthen our relationship."

The Most Complex Skill

Skills 1-3 handle most safety issues

Share intent, apologize, and contrast work when:

  • Your intent is misunderstood
  • You've made a mistake
  • There's a simple misunderstanding

But what if you're truly at cross purposes?

When you genuinely want different things, you need a more sophisticated approach...

Enter Skill 4: Create a Mutual Purpose

Skill 4: Create a Mutual Purpose

When you're at cross purposes, use CRIB

Commit to seek mutual purpose

Make a public commitment to stay in conversation until you find something that serves everyone

Recognize the purpose behind the strategy

Ask WHY they want what they're asking for. Separate demands from underlying purpose

Invent a mutual purpose

If still at odds, invent a higher or longer-term purpose that's more motivating

Brainstorm new strategies

With mutual purpose clear, jointly search for solutions that serve everyone

Commit to Seek Mutual Purpose

Stop fighting and start with heart

  • Stop using silence or violence to compel others
  • Suspend belief that your choice is the only one
  • Open your mind: maybe there's a third choice
  • Act on faith that your partner is stuck because they feel unsafe

Say It Out Loud

"It seems like we're both trying to force our view on each other. I commit to stay in this discussion until we have a solution that satisfies both of us."

Recognize the Purpose Behind the Strategy

We confuse wants (purpose) with strategy

Example: Movie vs. Stay Home

Strategies: "I want to go to a movie" vs. "I want to stay home"

Ask WHY:

"Why do you want to stay home?" → "Peace and quiet"

"Why do you want to go to a movie?" → "Time with you, away from kids"

Real purposes: Peace/quiet AND time together away

New option: Drive up the canyon together—quiet AND away!

Invent a Mutual Purpose

When purposes can't both be served

Move to more encompassing goals—find objectives that are more meaningful or rewarding

Example: Job Promotion Requiring Move

Your goal: Take promotion (career advancement)

Spouse's goal: Stay put (family stability, kids' friends)

Higher purpose: The needs of our relationship and children come before career aspirations

By focusing on higher and longer-term goals, you transcend short-term compromises

Brainstorm New Strategies

With mutual purpose clear, create options

  • Once safety is restored, return to content
  • Come up with options that serve everyone
  • Suspend judgment and think outside the box
  • You'll no longer spend energy on unproductive conflict

Questions to Ask

Can we find a local job that meets career goals? Is this the only opportunity? Is a move really necessary? Is there another community offering similar benefits?

Discussion Question

Which skill feels easiest and which feels hardest for you? Think of a time arguing over what to do instead of why—how could CRIB help you get unstuck in your next crucial conversation?

Discuss ~10 minutes.

Safety in Virtual Communication

Same principles, different medium

The core conditions don't change. You still need mutual purpose and mutual respect. But in written communication, it's even more essential to verbalize your intent.

The "Write It Twice" Rule

First: Write to get your content across

Second: Read slowly imagining their face. How might they feel? Rewrite with safety in mind.

Be Explicit About Intent

"If you could see my face right now, you'd see the wrinkles in my worried forehead as I hope this isn't coming across as harsh or critical..."

Putting It All Together

The complete process

Step 1: Notice safety is at risk

Watch for silence or violence

Step 2: Step out of content

Stop talking about the topic

Step 3: Decide which condition is at risk

Mutual Purpose or Mutual Respect?

Step 4: Use the appropriate skill(s)

Share Intent, Apologize, Contrast, or CRIB

Step 5: Step back into content

Once safety is restored, continue dialogue

Practice This Week

In your next crucial conversation:

  • Start with good intent: Share what you want for everyone up front
  • Watch for safety violations: Notice when respect or purpose is at risk
  • Step out when needed: Stop and rebuild safety before continuing
  • Use contrasting preventively: Clarify intent before misunderstandings occur

This Week's Challenge

Practice one conversation with these steps:

  • Plan a crucial conversation you need to have
  • Write out how you'll share your good intent at the start
  • Prepare a contrasting statement to prevent misunderstanding
  • If you get stuck, use CRIB to create mutual purpose

Remember

Safety ≠ Comfort

Crucial conversations require stretch and vulnerability. Safety means meaning flows freely, not that everyone feels comfortable.

It's About Intent

People don't become defensive about WHAT you say—they become defensive about WHY they think you're saying it.

Key Takeaway

When it's safe, you can talk about almost anything

Step out of the content when safety is at risk. Rebuild safety using the four skills. Then step back in and continue the dialogue.

People never become defensive about what you're saying—only about why they think you're saying it.
